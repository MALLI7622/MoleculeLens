\begin{abstract}
Drug--text retrieval systems can connect molecular structures to mechanism descriptions, but most current models are difficult to audit. They often rely on large black-box encoders, and benchmark scores can be inflated by memorized scaffolds, drug names, or target metadata. As a result, it is unclear whether a retrieved match is supported by meaningful molecular substructures or by easier lexical shortcuts. We present \textit{MoleculeLens}, an interpretable drug--text retrieval framework that makes each alignment decision traceable to salient molecular features. MoleculeLens aligns fixed molecular fingerprints and frozen biomedical text embeddings using learned linear projection heads. This simple design is the key contribution: the molecule-side linear map gives an efficient saliency score for each ECFP4 bit, allowing retrieval decisions to be inspected at the level of chemically meaningful substructures. On held-out scaffold pairs, this saliency proxy closely matches exact score-derived quantities, with mean Pearson correlation 0.905 against normalized-score gradients and 0.947 against leave-one-bit-out score drops. We evaluate MoleculeLens on a reproducible ChEMBL benchmark of 2{,}699 approved-drug mechanism pairs using a Bemis--Murcko scaffold-disjoint split. MoleculeLens achieves the strongest Recall@1 and MRR among the evaluated cross-modal baselines on this split, while also exposing when performance depends on drug-name or target/action metadata. Beyond retrieval accuracy, MoleculeLens provides pharmacophore-level explanations for correct matches, faithful diagnostics for failed retrievals, and a same-model substructure-level analysis of lexical leakage. These results show that lightweight linear bridges can make drug--text alignment more auditable without giving up competitive top-rank retrieval performance.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Drug--text alignment asks whether molecular structures and mechanism descriptions can be embedded in a common space for retrieval. The problem is useful for mechanism search, dataset curation, and model auditing, but it is hard to trust a high retrieval score by itself. Drug names, target labels, and scaffold-correlated train/test examples can make a model look accurate even when the molecular evidence behind a match is weak. Standard dual encoders also give little direct evidence about which substructures support a retrieved mechanism.

MoleculeLens addresses this gap with a deliberately constrained design. It freezes the molecule and text encoders, learns only thin linear projection heads, and uses the molecule-side linear map as a lens on ECFP4 fingerprint bits. The goal is not to build the largest drug--language model. The goal is to make drug--text retrieval auditable: each prediction can be traced back to chemically meaningful fingerprint environments, and shortcut behavior can be inspected at the substructure level.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{An auditable retrieval architecture.} MoleculeLens aligns fixed molecular fingerprints and frozen biomedical text embeddings with learned linear bridges, so retrieval remains competitive while the molecular side stays directly inspectable.
\item \textbf{Validated substructure saliency.} The linear molecular bridge yields a closed-form ECFP4 saliency proxy that closely matches exact normalized-score gradients and exact leave-one-bit-out score drops on held-out scaffold pairs.
\item \textbf{A scaffold-disjoint drug--mechanism benchmark.} We construct a reproducible ChEMBL benchmark of 2,699 approved-drug mechanism pairs and evaluate under Bemis--Murcko scaffold shift, with explicit controls for drug-name and target/action metadata.
\item \textbf{Audits beyond top-line accuracy.} We use the same lens to explain correct matches, diagnose failed retrievals, quantify same-model drug-name leakage as substructure shifts, and localize drug-retrievable text signal with a descriptive logit-lens diagnostic.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Related work and positioning.}
Drug--text and molecule--language models include retrieval-oriented systems such as Text2Mol~\citep{edwards2021text2mol}, molecular language models such as MolT5~\citep{edwards2022molt5} and BioT5~\citep{pei2023biot5}, graph-language systems such as MolCA~\citep{liu2023molca}, and multimodal biomedical pretraining such as KV-PLM~\citep{zeng2022kvplm}. Graphormer~\citep{ying2021graphormer} and Uni-Mol~\citep{zhou2023unimol} are important molecular representation baselines, though not direct text-query retrieval systems without additional alignment. MoleculeLens is complementary to these higher-capacity models: it emphasizes scaffold-disjoint drug--mechanism retrieval and auditable substructure-level explanations. The evaluation design follows the molecular-learning practice of scaffold splits~\citep{bemis1996frameworks} and combines it with text leakage controls. The interpretability component is related to linear feature analysis and attribution-graph work~\citep{bricken2023monosemanticity,templeton2024monosemanticity,lindsey2025attribution}, but our features are chemically grounded from the start because ECFP4 bits correspond to circular molecular environments~\citep{rogers2010ecfp} recoverable with RDKit~\citep{rdkit}.

\section{Main Contributions}
\label{sec:main-contributions}

\paragraph{Strategy.}
Figure~\ref{fig:architecture-main} summarizes the central strategy. MoleculeLens does not attempt to interpret every hidden unit of a large multimodal model. Instead, it inserts a small linear bridge between a fixed molecular representation and a frozen biomedical text encoder. That bridge creates a shared retrieval space and, because the molecule-side map is linear, a direct route from each retrieval score back to ECFP4 substructures.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \centering
  \makebox[\linewidth][c]{\includegraphics[width=1.08\linewidth]{fig_architecture.pdf}}
  \caption{\textbf{MoleculeLens strategy.} Frozen ECFP4 and biomedical text encoders are connected by learned linear projection heads and a CLOOB/InfoLOOB retrieval objective. The molecule-side linear map enables direct ECFP4-bit saliency; the text-side projection is reused for the layer-wise logit-lens diagnostic.}
  \label{fig:architecture-main}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Linear bridge retrieval model.}
For drug $i$, RDKit computes a radius-2, 2048-bit ECFP4 fingerprint $x_i$~\citep{rogers2010ecfp}. For text $i$, a frozen biomedical sentence encoder produces a CLS-style embedding $z_i$. MoleculeLens learns only two linear projection heads,
\begin{equation}
b_i^{(m)} = W_m x_i + b_m,\qquad b_i^{(t)}=W_t z_i+b_t,
\end{equation}
followed by $L_2$ normalization and cosine scoring. Training uses symmetric InfoNCE with same-target weighting and a hardest-same-target margin term. The scaffold model uses batch size 512, learning rate $10^{-3}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$, temperature 0.07, margin 0.1, and 100 epochs. All encoders are frozen; only $W_m,b_m,W_t,b_t$ are trained. Training the projection heads takes under 30 seconds per run on a single NVIDIA GPU; ECFP4 attribution and contrastive logit-lens analyses each complete in under 10 minutes. The dominant cost is the one-time frozen text encoding shared across experiments. Additional implementation details are in Appendix~\ref{app:architecture}.

\paragraph{Substructure saliency from the molecular bridge.}
The normalized retrieval score is
\begin{equation}
s_{ij}=\hat{b}_i^{(m)}\cdot\hat{b}_j^{(t)}
=\mathrm{normalize}(W_m x_i+b_m)\cdot\mathrm{normalize}(W_t z_j+b_t).
\end{equation}
Because $W_m$ is linear, we use the closed-form ECFP4 saliency proxy
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:attribution}
\mathrm{attr}_k(i,j)=\bigl[W_m^\top\hat{b}_j^{(t)}\bigr]_k,
\end{equation}
where $\hat{b}_j^{(t)}=\mathrm{normalize}(W_tz_j+b_t)$. Equation~\ref{eq:attribution} is a fast ranking proxy rather than an exact causal decomposition: the exact normalized score also depends on the molecule-side bias and $\|W_mx_i+b_m\|$. This is why the experimental section validates it against exact gradients and exact leave-one-bit-out score drops before using it for chemical interpretation.

\paragraph{Diagnostics enabled by the same bridge.}
The same linear bottleneck supports three audits. First, saliency over present ECFP4 bits lets us inspect correct and incorrect retrievals as substructure-level decisions. Second, removing only the explicit drug-name span at inference while holding model weights fixed tests whether lexical leakage changes which molecular features are salient. Third, applying the trained text bridge to each hidden layer of the frozen text encoder gives a descriptive contrastive logit lens for locating where drug-retrievable text information becomes available.

\section{Experimental Validation}
\label{sec:experiments}

\paragraph{Dataset and task.}
We construct the benchmark from ChEMBL~\citep{zdrazil2024chembl} by filtering for approved drugs (\texttt{max\_phase=4}), valid mechanism text, and parseable canonical SMILES. The final dataset contains 2,699 drug--text pairs spanning 1,196 unique Bemis--Murcko scaffolds. The primary text field concatenates mechanism of action, target name, action type, and drug name:
\begin{equation}
\texttt{text\_rich}=\texttt{mechanism}+\texttt{target}+\texttt{action}+\texttt{drug}.
\end{equation}
The main split uses seed 0 over unique scaffolds: 2,019 train examples, 245 validation examples, and 435 held-out test examples whose scaffolds are unseen during training. We report text-query retrieval against a molecule gallery using Recall@1, MRR, Recall@5, and Recall@10 for apples-to-apples comparison with baselines. All main-table rows use single-label diagonal evaluation: a prediction is counted correct only when the exact matching row is returned. This is intentionally conservative for drugs with multiple ChEMBL mechanism rows, but it is the only consistent apples-to-apples protocol for baselines where per-example ranked lists are not available.

Several test drugs appear under multiple target--action rows. For attribution analyses, same-drug and wrong-but-close filtering use RDKit \texttt{FragmentParent} standardisation over SMILES to avoid treating salt or formulation variants as independent structures. In the canonical scaffold test set, 193 of 435 rows belong to 76 parent structures with multiple mechanism rows.

\paragraph{What substructures recur?}
The saliency lens is useful only if it identifies interpretable molecular features rather than a single global shortcut. Appendix Table~\ref{tab:universal-bits} summarizes the most frequent high-saliency ECFP4 bits across the scaffold test set. Bit 1538 is the most common, appearing in the top-10 for 85 pairs; RDKit decoding maps it to a benzene-ring environment with at least one heteroatom substituent at radius 2, a common pharmacophore anchor across several mechanism families. Frequencies drop sharply after the leading bits, and several frequent bits have near-zero mean signed attribution because they appear in both positive and negative contexts. This pattern supports the intended interpretation: MoleculeLens exposes recurring chemically meaningful substructures, but retrieval decisions are not dominated by one universal fingerprint bit.

\paragraph{Auditing near misses.}
Faithful attribution is useful beyond correctly retrieved examples. Among rank-2/3 errors after parent-structure filtering, 30 of 43 have attribution correlation above 0.5 between the correct drug and the retrieved drug, with mean correlation 0.569. These are not counted as correct in the diagonal retrieval metric, but the analysis distinguishes chemically plausible near misses from arbitrary failures. Figure~\ref{fig:wrong-close-main} shows a representative case: sodium phenylacetate is ranked second while sodium phenylbutyrate is ranked first, and the two molecules share the same ammonia-lowering mechanism with highly overlapping salient substructures. This does not make the retrieval correct, but it shows that MoleculeLens can expose when an error is driven by related chemical evidence rather than an opaque embedding artifact.

\paragraph{Same-model leakage attribution.}
We next ask whether the same rich model uses the same substructures when the explicit drug name is removed at inference. For each test pair, let $\mathcal{T}^{\mathrm{rich}}_i$ and $\mathcal{T}^{\mathrm{nodrug}}_i$ be the top-10 saliency-bit sets under the original query and the same query with \texttt{Drug: X.} removed. We compute
\begin{equation}
J_i=\frac{|\mathcal{T}^{\mathrm{rich}}_i\cap\mathcal{T}^{\mathrm{nodrug}}_i|}{|\mathcal{T}^{\mathrm{rich}}_i\cup\mathcal{T}^{\mathrm{nodrug}}_i|}.
\end{equation}
Mean overlap is only $0.356\pm0.161$; 82/435 pairs have $J_i<0.2$, 36/435 have $J_i>0.6$, and 13/435 are leakage candidates that are correct under \texttt{text\_rich}, wrong under the same-model no-name query, and have $J_i<0.2$. This converts the score-level leakage drop into a structural claim: for a substantial subset of test pairs, removing the name changes which molecular features drive retrieval even though model weights are fixed.

\subsection{Layer-Wise Text Diagnostic}

We adapt the logit-lens idea~\citep{voss2020logitlens} by applying the trained text projection head $W_t$ to the CLS state at each S-Biomed-RoBERTa layer. The diagnostic exactly replays the main rich model at layer 12: the reconstructed embeddings match the saved outputs within $1.21\times10^{-3}$ maximum absolute error and recover R@1~=~0.225. Layers 0--11 are near chance for both rich and retrained no-drug text (R@1 between 0.002 and 0.011), while layer 12 jumps to R@1~=~0.225 for \texttt{text\_rich} and 0.113 for \texttt{text\_nodrug}. This is best viewed as a descriptive sanity check: retrieval signal and the rich-vs-no-name gap both appear in the final contextualized CLS representation, not gradually across intermediate layers. The full layer table and attention visualizations are in Appendix~\ref{app:logit-lens}.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

MoleculeLens is not a claim that frozen ECFP4 plus a linear bridge solves scaffold-generalized drug--text retrieval. Its narrower contribution is that a lightweight model remains competitive at top-rank scaffold retrieval while making its decisions inspectable at the ECFP4-substructure level. The saliency proxy is validated against exact score-derived quantities, remains faithful on retrieval failures, and exposes same-model drug-name leakage as a shift in salient molecular features.

The benchmark has important limitations. The rich text field includes target and action metadata, and the mechanism-only ablation confirms that these fields contribute retrieval signal; the rich-text result should therefore be interpreted as curated mechanism-metadata retrieval rather than pure free-text mechanism understanding. Implicit lexical cues, such as proprietary-name fragments embedded in mechanism text, are not removed by the no-drug ablation and may still inflate no-drug scores. The dataset covers FDA-approved drugs with curated ChEMBL mechanisms and does not establish generalization to investigational compounds or non-ChEMBL prose. Finally, the baseline set does not include adapted MolT5, BioT5, Uni-Mol, MolCA, or chemistry-LLM retrieval systems; these require nontrivial task adaptation and remain important future comparisons.

\paragraph{Reproducibility and intended use.}
The benchmark is designed to make these limitations visible rather than hide them. The scaffold split is released as a fixed artifact, the paper-facing tables are regenerated through a single artifact-refresh path, and all main numbers are tied to saved manifests. This matters because retrieval benchmarks are easy to contaminate: changing from scaffold-held-out evaluation to a full-gallery setting, or changing from same-model ablation to retrained no-drug comparison, answers a different scientific question. We therefore report the stronger rich-text result, the stricter mechanism-only condition, and the same-model no-name ablation side by side.

MoleculeLens is intended as an auditing tool for drug--text alignment, not as a standalone decision system for chemistry or medicine. It does not generate new molecules and should not be used for clinical or regulatory decisions without expert review. Its positive use case is narrower: when a retrieval model proposes a structure--mechanism match, MoleculeLens can show which fingerprint environments contributed to that match and whether those environments change under text perturbations. That makes it useful for diagnosing shortcut learning, prioritizing cases for medicinal-chemist inspection, and building stronger future benchmarks where higher-capacity encoders can be evaluated with the same scaffold and leakage controls.

\paragraph{Future interpretability directions.}
The shared 256-dimensional embedding space also creates a natural target for sparse autoencoder analysis~\citep{templeton2024monosemanticity,bricken2023monosemanticity} to test whether alignment dimensions are monosemantic or polysemantic. A cross-modal crosscoder~\citep{lindsey2025attribution} trained on paired molecule--text embeddings could further separate features that genuinely align structure and mechanism from features that mostly encode text-only leakage. These extensions are outside the present benchmark, but they follow directly from the linear-bridge design: MoleculeLens supplies a reproducible scaffold split, saved embeddings, and substructure-level saliency targets that stronger encoders and richer interpretability tools can reuse.

\paragraph{Benchmark scope.}
The benchmark covers FDA-approved drugs with curated ChEMBL mechanism text, deliberately including entries with parseable SMILES and non-empty mechanism descriptions. It does not cover investigational compounds, natural products without approved status, or mechanism texts written in a different domain from the training distribution of S-Biomed-RoBERTa. Same-modality text lookup controls are also not treated as competing baselines because they access gallery text rather than molecular structure; they are better interpreted as text-identifiability audits reported separately from cross-modal retrieval.

\section{Conclusion}

On a 2,699-pair ChEMBL benchmark with scaffold-disjoint evaluation, MoleculeLens outperforms MolPrompt and KV-PLM on Recall@1 and MRR while clearly exposing the role of lexical and target/action metadata. Its central value is interpretability: the linear molecular bridge yields a validated, efficient ECFP4 saliency lens that can audit correct matches, failed retrievals, and same-model leakage. This makes MoleculeLens a useful foundation for future work on stronger encoders, text-identifiability audits, and feature-level analysis of drug--text alignment.
